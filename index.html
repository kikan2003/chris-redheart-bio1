Christopher Francis Redheart – A Satirical Biography

Christopher Francis Redheart was born in Edinburgh in 1962, on a grey and stormy day that promised little, for him or for the world. His mother, Lena, a Scotswoman with a character as unyielding as the mountains surrounding her home, and his father, Frederich, a Frenchman with the melancholy of those who see the world through the prism of red wine and lost memories, bestowed upon him the name "Christopher Francis." However, as we shall later discover, the poor Christopher was never particularly convinced of his own name, nor of the life that awaited him.

Raised in a home that, by his own account, resembled more a Thomas Hardy novel than a Disney movie, Christopher—or "Chris," as the true friends (and, according to him, also the false ones) called him—did not take long to rebel against the tranquil life in Scotland and seek out the vast array of experiences the world had to offer. In 1979, he joined the navy, where he was discharged the following year for insubordination and disobedience. That same year, 1980, he set off on a journey to the USA. The inspiration, supposedly, came from the Supertramp album “Breakfast in America,” although it could also have been due to the shame of his school peers. The trip lasted 15 months and saw him take on the most bizarre and unexpected jobs one could imagine.

In Miami, he worked as a bouncer, his intimidating talents limited to his ability to drink more than reasonably expected while offering philosophical commentary on fate’s morality. In Atlanta, his life took an even more unexpected turn when he worked as a gigolo, although the specifics of his services remained vague, as he was always evasive when asked. In Joplin, Missouri, he claims to have taught French, although neither he nor anyone in Joplin could verify if he actually spoke the language. In Amarillo, Texas, he not only worked as a trapeze artist but also as a clown in a circus that was as disorganized as his life. Finally, in San Diego, he was expelled after a dramatic fall from his post as a firefighter, an event involving women, alcohol, and the burning of hundreds of acres of forest. Fortunately, there were no fatalities, but a fractured reputation, which allowed him to do what he did best: run away.

After his tumultuous time in the USA, Redheart returned to Europe, where, in 1982, he embarked on a tour of Europe, a chapter in his life filled with more adventures. In Paris, the promised land for rebellious young men, he arrived with dreams of becoming a writer. Yet, he was detained after an incident allegedly involving a scandalous dispute over a woman—though Redheart would deny this with a shrug, suggesting that the police, as always, exaggerated the story. In Bruges, he found himself arrested after a barroom brawl involving dice, a Tibetan monk, and a handsome flower vendor. Redheart claimed it was a misunderstanding. In Hamburg, he was accused of "violations of morality," an event tied to a mysterious picture of Jesus Christ and a white horse. The details remain unclear, but the urban legend endures.

In 1983, Chris returned to England and pursued a degree in Marine Engineering at Liverpool. The studies, which he completed without much enthusiasm, seemed to have no lasting impact on him, though they would prove to be somewhat ironic, as he would never use his engineering knowledge in his later life. In 1988, he finally settled in Aberdeen, where he has lived ever since with his wife Eileen and their four children. He began working in the marine industry, a field he was never truly satisfied with but which, ironically, he could never escape.

Today, Chris works for a private company and writes in his private island retreat, a place where, one imagines, he still seeks to untangle the stories of his extraordinary life. Who is Christopher Francis Redheart? Well, it depends on whom you ask. A man whose face, in his own words, was "molded by the wind and storms, but never by reason," a traveler who has left more questions than answers. A writer who, deep down, seeks a story he will never be able to tell entirely—or at least not with precision. A man who has been everywhere and, in the end, has found that the only place left for him to know is perhaps the same one that resides within his own mind. But don’t worry, he’ll probably sort it out soon, with a cigarette, a whisky, and some bewildering tale.
